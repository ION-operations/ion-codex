## 1. Core thesis

ION is a continuity-governed AI operating substrate.

At the deepest level, ION is not just an app, assistant, workflow runner, or scheduler.
It is a lawful continuity protocol that determines how intelligence persists, routes, acts, hands off, reviews itself, evolves future work, and remains governable across time, carriers, and execution modes.

The central claim of the system is:

> Intelligence inside an operational environment should not be modeled as one-off prompt response.
> It should be modeled as lawful continuity.

ION therefore treats intelligence as something that must:
- preserve context lawfully,
- route action through bounded process,
- maintain continuity across steps,
- carry future work in structured form,
- remain legible and handoff-capable,
- and operate under explicit authority, review, threshold, and enactment law.

## 2. Project-level vision

The long-term vision is to build an AI-native operating substrate capable of powering:
- an IDE-native working intelligence,
- structured autonomous or semi-autonomous execution,
- bounded multi-agent orchestration,
- future-aware planning and enactment,
- lawful handoff and continuation,
- and eventually a broader operating environment in which intelligence is not bolted onto tools, but is part of the governing runtime itself.

In that end-state, the system is not merely using a model as an external helper.
It is organizing intelligence as a lawful operational field.

## 4. What ION fundamentally is

### 4.1 ION as the protocol of continuity

ION is the loop that ensures work done by intelligence is not an isolated response but part of a governed, continuous, lawful process.

The irreducible loop is:
1. context is compiled,
2. the next lawful route is determined,
3. continuity is carried in bounded form,
4. execution occurs through allowed carriers,
5. review, threshold, and receipts preserve legitimacy,
6. future work remains structured rather than lost,
7. and the system can continue without hidden memory dependence.

### 4.2 ION as lawful intelligence infrastructure

ION is infrastructure for intelligence behavior.
It does not merely attach an LLM to tools.
It defines how intelligence should exist inside an operational system.

### 4.3 ION as the loop beneath embodiments

Builds, branches, names, modules, and packaging may change.
The invariant center should remain:
- lawful continuity,
- lawful routing,
- lawful enactment,
- lawful future structure,
- and lawful continuation.

## 5. Kernel versus scheduler versus runtime

This distinction must remain explicit.

### 5.1 Kernel

The kernel is the lawful substrate.
It owns:
- authority,
- continuity,
- packet law,
- horizon law,
- enactment law,
- threshold and review law,
- recovery and replay law,
- and carrier constraints.

### 5.2 Scheduler

The scheduler is a subsystem inside kernel law.
It does not replace the kernel.

It decides, under law:
- what should run now,
- what should remain provisional,
- what should tighten,
- what should wait,
- which carrier should execute,
- and how future schedule shape updates as present work compiles.

After L0, scheduler law is explicit but still first-pass.
Schedule state, commitment posture, horizon bridging, schedule projection, and schedule receipts are now real.
Executor selection is not yet principled until L1 executor capability law lands.

### 5.3 Runtime

The runtime is the live operational field in which kernel law is enacted:
- supervised daemon service,
- operator CLI and IDE surface,
- execution bridges,
- workflow state,
- receipts,
- and later scheduler-guided execution.

## 6. Major system components

### 6.1 Context compilation system

Compiles the relevant bounded context needed for lawful routing and execution.

### 6.2 Routing system

Determines the next lawful route through work, including question handling, child-work issuance, follow-up, review, and continuation.

### 6.3 Packet system

Provides bounded continuity carriers.
Packets are how the system externalizes state for lawful continuation, handoff, fallback, and takeover.

### 6.4 Horizon system

Holds future work pressure across immediate, near, and far horizons.
This is how future structure exists without collapsing into either rigid planning or vague intention.

### 6.5 Tightening system

Determines when future work can move closer to execution readiness.
It compresses possibility into lawful next candidates.

### 6.6 Enactment system

Turns packet-ready tightened candidates into canonical executable packet artifacts.
This returns future pressure into the main loop.

### 6.7 Receipt and traceability system

Preserves durable traceability for major transitions such as enactment and scheduling decisions.

### 6.8 Review and threshold system

Determines where work must pause, escalate, seek review, or remain non-final.
This is essential for safe autonomy.

### 6.9 Recovery and replay system

Allows lawful continuation after interruption, error, or partial completion.

### 6.10 Executor and carrier system

Defines who or what can carry a step:
- human operator,
- supervised automation,
- external or API carrier,
- child agent,
- and later bounded multi-agent executors.

### 6.11 Scheduler and orchestration system

This is the future orchestration intelligence of the kernel.
It progressively compiles the future schedule under law rather than treating it as static plan or improvisation.

### 6.12 IDE and operating surface

This is how the system becomes usable:
- IDE-native commands,
- operating context,
- runtime control,
- packet generation,
- task continuation,
- orchestration visibility,
- and later broader OS-native integration.

## 7. How the system is expected to operate in practice

### 7.1 Operator or developer enters the system

They invoke the kernel through the operator surface, IDE, or later higher-order environment.

### 7.2 Context is compiled

Relevant state, packet history, workflow context, horizon pressure, and required reads are assembled.

### 7.3 The next lawful route is determined

The system decides the next action under continuity law.
This may involve:
- direct execution,
- packet generation,
- question routing,
- child-work issuance,
- review,
- follow-up,
- horizon tightening,
- or schedule projection.

### 7.4 Execution is carried through lawful carriers

A step may be carried by:
- the human operator,
- supervised runtime,
- external bridge,
- or later another executor.

Changing the carrier should not change the process law.

### 7.5 Receipts and continuity artifacts are preserved

The system leaves behind lawful evidence of what occurred, enough for continuation, auditability, and takeover.

### 7.6 Future work is not lost

Unfinished or future pressure is retained through horizon structures and later scheduling structures.

### 7.7 The system continues

The result is not that a model answered.
The result is that the continuity loop remains alive and governable.

## 8. The IDE and operating-substrate vision

### 8.1 IDE-native intelligence

The system is expected to power an IDE in which intelligence is not merely autocomplete or chat.
Instead, the IDE becomes a lawful continuity environment where:
- work is packetized,
- tasks continue across sessions,
- horizon work becomes executable,
- executors can lawfully take over,
- and the operator can supervise a living orchestration field.

### 8.2 Operating-substrate vision

Beyond the IDE, the long-term direction points toward an operating environment where intelligence becomes part of the runtime fabric.
That means:
- task continuity across contexts,
- future-aware orchestration,
- lawful dynamic scheduling,
- system-level agent coordination,
- durable state and handoff,
- and a higher-order substrate for AI-native work.

### 8.3 Why this matters

Most current AI systems are attached to tools.
This project is heading toward tools being attached to a lawful intelligence substrate.

## 9. The scheduler as central architecture

### 9.1 Not a normal scheduler

This is not just a queue, dispatcher, planner, or worker pool.
It is a lawful horizon-aware orchestration scheduler.

### 9.2 Progressive schedule compilation

The schedule is neither fully fixed nor fully improvised.
It is progressively compiled as present work changes lawful future possibility.

### 9.3 Horizon-sensitive rigidity

A mature scheduler should operate with different rigidity by horizon:
- immediate window: mostly frozen,
- near window: structured but revisable,
- far window: weakly frozen possibility field.

### 9.4 Commitment gradient

The future schedule needs commitment states such as:
- speculative,
- emerging,
- likely,
- precommitted,
- committed,
- enacted,
- and completed.

L0 now provides first-pass commitment posture and schedule state surfaces.
What remains is deeper executor-aware and branch-aware law rather than mere naming.

### 9.5 Executor-aware orchestration

The scheduler must reason about executor capability, trust, carrier type, fallback, and continuity cost.

### 9.6 Branch-aware orchestration

The future is not one line.
It may include:
- current path,
- near conditional branches,
- farther strategic possibility structures,
- and later merge and settlement work.

### 9.7 Why scheduler law matters

Without explicit scheduler law, the kernel remains powerful but incomplete.
With it, the system can become a true orchestration substrate.
