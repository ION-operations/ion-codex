## Why lifecycle is the right paired follow-up

Pass 39 established activation authority as the review-layer center that decides whether candidate work may cross into executable enactment.

What still remained missing was the matched surface that answers:

**Once enactment is authorized, how is an executor lawfully claimed, prepared, entered, suspended, resumed, released, returned, failed, or retired?**

That question is too important to leave distributed across scheduler law, handoff packets, runtime-state receipts, and carrier-specific shell behavior.
But it is also too easy to over-expand into a fake manager ontology.
So the correct next step is a quarantined lifecycle draft.

## What this packet contains

1. a review-layer draft of `EXECUTOR_LIFECYCLE_PROTOCOL`
2. a review matrix listing unresolved choices and pass/fail checks
3. a boundary statement that keeps activation authority, continuation ontology, and runtime-session law separate

## Review questions that must now become explicit

The draft should survive review on the following questions:

1. **Jurisdiction** — does executor lifecycle actually govern claim/readiness/entry/suspend/resume/release/retire transitions without swallowing activation authority?
2. **Runtime boundary** — does the draft avoid turning runtime-state witness or session queue surfaces into lifecycle ontology?
3. **Carrier neutrality** — can manual, IDE, daemon, API, and later bounded multi-carrier enactment share one lifecycle law?
4. **Settlement truth** — do failure, return, release, and retirement semantics preserve continuity and settlement truth rather than orphaning work?
5. **Anti-theater discipline** — does the draft refuse role inflation and unattended fleet mythology while still allowing bounded executor transitions?

## Current best judgment

The lifecycle center is now precise enough for a review draft.
It is **not** yet precise enough for active-law installation.

The maturity signal here is that lifecycle law can now say what it governs after activation, while refusing to become activation authority, runtime-session ontology, or role theater.
