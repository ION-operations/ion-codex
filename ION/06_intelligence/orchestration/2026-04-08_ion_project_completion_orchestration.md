# ION Project Completion Orchestration

## Completion statement

ION reaches completion when the same canonical workflow can be carried by:
- a single interactive agent,
- an IDE agent,
- the supervised runtime,
- an external/API worker,
- and later a bounded swarm,

without changing the law of context compilation, bounded execution, proposal return, governed landing, state update, and next-handoff emission.

## What completion is not

Completion is not:
- maximum protocol count,
- maximum witness depth,
- maximum number of services,
- or the absence of further research.

Completion is a state where the organism is executable, teachable, resumable, extensible, and trustworthy.

## Endgame image

A future-capable executor enters the repo and can discover:
1. what the organism is trying to do,
2. what state is authoritative,
3. what the next bounded step is,
4. what exact packet or template must be used,
5. what outputs must be returned,
6. what gates must be passed,
7. what the near and far horizon currently are,
8. how the scheduler is shaping the next work,
9. and how another executor could continue seamlessly after it.

## Strategic principles for the rest of the project

1. **One workflow only**
   No future work may introduce a convenience path that is not reducible to the canonical loop.

2. **Carrier symmetry first**
   If an automated step cannot be carried manually under the same packet law, it is not complete.

3. **Horizon before haste**
   The system should not only know what to do next. It should know what is likely next and what later chains are beginning to take shape.

4. **Rehearsal before claim**
   New orchestration claims should be backed by scenario tests or rehearsals, not only module-unit proof.

5. **Fresh executor criterion**
   Every major milestone should improve the ability of a fresh executor to continue from lawful outputs alone.

6. **Support layers remain subordinate**
   Witness, reporting, browse, trace, and summary layers remain useful but never outrank kernel truth or workflow law.

7. **Scheduler law must become explicit before scale**
   Horizon state, enactment, and receipts already imply a scheduler. Before the repo widens into richer executor selection or swarm work, scheduling intelligence must be named, bounded, and made legible.

8. **Takeover proof before orchestration scale**
   Fresh-executor continuation is a release gate for any broader carrier or multi-agent claims.

9. **Plans must reconcile with landed packet history**
   The orchestration stack must always say what is actually landed, what is next, and how local packet work feeds the larger phase architecture.

## Scheduler insertion point

The dynamic orchestration scheduler should not be treated as a late optional polish layer.

It is the next major architecture center after K7 because:
- K3 made future pressure durable,
- K4 returned packet-ready future pressure into the loop,
- K5 made enactment traceable,
- K6 proved those parts together,
- and K7 will test whether another executor can continue from those bounded artifacts.

Once K7 lands, the repo will have enough proof to formalize scheduling law without hand-waving.

## Completion workstreams

### Workstream A — Workflow core perfection
Objective:
Make the workflow itself easier to execute than to misunderstand.

Expected outputs:
- workflow/module map kept current,
- standardized bounded packet families,
- stronger continuation proofs,
- explicit canonical examples.

### Workstream B — Horizon orchestration
Objective:
Turn immediate/near/far orchestration into a real maintained kernel capability rather than only doctrine.

Expected outputs:
- horizon state families,
- tightening/update helpers,
- enactment bridge back into packet law,
- enactment receipts,
- future-window projections,
- and rehearsal-backed horizon behavior.

### Workstream C — Carrier neutrality and takeover
Objective:
Prove that carrier changes and executor changes do not change the process.

Expected outputs:
- executor capability registry,
- takeover packet contracts,
- carrier-equivalence scenarios,
- fresh-executor continuation proofs.

### Workstream D — Multi-agent law
Objective:
Scale bounded work without losing the one-loop law.

Expected outputs:
- bounded parallelism and settlement law definition,
- bounded allocator/fan-out rules,
- merge/review settlement law,
- branch horizon semantics,
- anti-drift and budget controls.

### Workstream E — Lawful orchestration scheduler
Objective:
Make scheduling intelligence explicit before the repo widens into richer executor selection and bounded swarm operation.

Expected outputs:
- scheduler doctrine and state model,
- commitment gradient semantics,
- horizon-to-schedule bridge,
- scheduling receipts,
- arbitration policy surface,
- carrier-binding law,
- and retry/stale/reassignment rules.

Why it now matters:
The scheduler is already implied by horizon state, tightening, enactment, and receipts. Making it explicit prevents later executor-registry and swarm work from depending on hidden scheduling intuition.
